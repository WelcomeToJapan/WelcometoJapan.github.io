Japanese Etiquette & Culture

A simple guide for a trip to Japan.

Greetings and Social Etiquette

Courtesy and respect are a part of daily Japanese life, though hopefully they are of yours too! Understanding certain customs can make a big difference.

  • Bowing: A simple head nod or a shallow bow is a great way to show respect or gratitude.
  • Giving & Receiving: Always use two hands when exchanging items like business cards.
  • Public Behavior: Keep your voice down on public transport and follow queuing etiquette.

Dining Customs

Japanese meals are a wonderful experience, and a few key phrases and rules will help you feel right at home.

  • Before & After: Say "itadakimasu" before you eat and "gochisousama deshita" after.
  • Chopsticks: Never stick them upright in your rice or pass food from one set to another.
  • Noodles: It's polite to slurp your noodles - it is meant improve the taste, and tbh after years here I have begun to believe it myself. Maybe its something about letting the air into your palate?

Onsen (Hot Springs) Etiquette

A trip to an onsen is a must, but remember these rules for a respectful and relaxing experience.

  • Get Naked: You must be completely nude to enter the bathing area. For first-timers, this might be a nerve-wracking experience, but after a few goes it becomes totally normal. No one is looking, no one cares, so just get over yourself and relax! Or get a private onsen.
  • Wash First: You must wash your body thoroughly at a shower stall before entering the bath.
  • Tattoos: Many onsen do not allow tattoos, though smaller ones may be covered with a patch.

Gift-Giving (Omiyage)

The tradition of omiyage is a thoughtful gesture. It's common to bring back gifts from a specific location to share with others. Said more simply, it's souvenirs. Omiyage are souvenirs. Buy souvenirs. Bring small gifts if you are visiting someone. It's not rocket science.
