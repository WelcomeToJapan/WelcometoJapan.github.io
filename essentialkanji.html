Essential Kanji for Travelers

You don't need to be fluent, but learning these few characters will make your trip much smoother.

駅

Station

Eki (えき)

You'll see this everywhere, from major Shinkansen hubs to tiny local train stops. Look for it to find your way to the trains.

入口

Entrance

Iriguchi (いりぐち)

The way in. Essential for finding the entrance to train stations, museums, temples, and shops.

出口

Exit

Deguchi (でぐち)

The way out. Just as important as knowing how to get in! Often followed by a number for specific station exits.

円

Yen

En (えん)

The symbol for Japan's currency. You will see this on every price tag in every shop and restaurant.

無料

Free of Charge

Muryō (むりょう)

One of the best words to see! It means something is free, like a city map, a WiFi hotspot, or a cup of tea.

有料

Fee Required

Yūryō (ゆうりょう)

The opposite of free. This indicates you have to pay for a service, like a highway, parking lot, or special exhibition.

営業中

Open

Eigyōchū (えいぎょうちゅう)

You'll see this sign hanging outside restaurants and shops to let you know they are open for business.

準備中

Preparing

Junbichū (じゅんびちゅう)

While it literally means "in preparation," this sign effectively means "closed," usually between lunch and dinner service.

禁止

Prohibited

Kinshi (きんし)

A very important word. It means "don't do this." You'll see it paired with other characters for "No Smoking" (喫煙禁止) or "No Entry" (立入禁止).

お手洗

Toilet / Washroom

Otearai (おてあらい)

The more polite term for a toilet. You'll often see this, or the Katakana version トイレ (Toire).

男

Man

Otoko (おとこ)

Essential for finding the correct public restroom. Usually seen on a blue curtain or sign.

女

Woman

Onna (おんな)

The character for "woman," used for restrooms and other gender-specific facilities. Usually on a red or pink sign.
