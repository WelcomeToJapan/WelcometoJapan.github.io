Keigo: Speaking Formally in Japanese

A Guide for Intermediate and Advanced Learners

Levels of Formality in Japanese

The Japanese language employs a complex and nuanced system of politeness levels that go far beyond a simple formal/informal dichotomy. These levels are determined by the relationship between the speaker, the listener, and any third parties being discussed. Understanding this system is crucial for social and professional success in Japan. The politeness spectrum can be broadly categorized into three levels:

  • Casual Language (タメ口 - Tamego): Used with close friends, family, and people of a lower social standing. It is characterized by the use of plain verb forms.
  • Polite Language (丁寧語 - Teineigo): The standard level of politeness taught to most beginners. It is marked by the use of です and ます forms. It is a neutral form of respect suitable for daily interactions with strangers or colleagues.
  • Honorific Language (敬語 - Keigo): The most formal level, which is itself divided into subcategories. It is used to show deference and respect, and its correct application is a hallmark of linguistic and cultural fluency.

What is Keigo (敬語)?

Keigo is not simply "polite" Japanese; it is a sophisticated linguistic system used to express respect and humility. It changes the vocabulary and grammar you use based on social hierarchy, group affiliation (うち-そと, uchi-soto), and context. Mastering Keigo is essential for functioning in professional environments in Japan. It is primarily divided into three categories:

1. Sonkeigo (尊敬語) - Respectful Language

This is used when speaking about a superior or someone you wish to show respect to (e.g., a customer, a senior manager). It elevates the other person and their actions. For example, instead of saying the president will 見る (miru - to see), you would say ご覧になる (goran ni naru).

2. Kenjōgo (謙譲語) - Humble Language

This is used when speaking about yourself or someone in your in-group to a superior or customer. It lowers your position or the position of your group, thereby elevating the listener. For example, when telling a client you will visit them, instead of 行く (iku - to go), you would use 伺う (ukagau).

3. Teineigo (丁寧語) - Polite Language

As mentioned, this is the foundation of polite speech using です and ます. It shows general respect to the listener without the hierarchical nuances of Sonkeigo or Kenjōgo. It makes your entire speech sound formal and refined.

When is Keigo Used?

The use of Keigo is not optional in certain contexts. Its absence can be perceived as rude, unprofessional, or even offensive. Key situations requiring Keigo include:

  • Business Settings: When interacting with clients, customers, suppliers, or senior colleagues and management.
  • Customer Service: Any employee speaking to a customer will use Keigo.
  • Formal Occasions: Speeches, ceremonies, academic presentations, and formal interviews.
  • Addressing Superiors: Speaking to anyone of a higher age or social rank, such as teachers, doctors, or government officials.
