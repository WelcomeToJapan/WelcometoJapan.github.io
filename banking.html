Banking in Japan

A guide to opening a bank account and managing your finances in Japan.

Navigating the Japanese Financial System

Understanding banking procedures is crucial for smooth daily life in Japan.

Opening a bank account in Japan is a fundamental step for anyone planning to live, work, or study long-term in the country. While the process can sometimes feel bureaucratic, especially for non-Japanese speakers, it's a necessary step for receiving salaries, paying bills, and managing daily expenses.

This guide will provide an overview of the types of banks, the documents typically required, and the general steps involved in setting up your finances in Japan.

Types of Banks in Japan

Mega Banks (City Banks)

Large, national banks with extensive branch and ATM networks. Examples include MUFG Bank, SMBC (Sumitomo Mitsui Banking Corporation), and Mizuho Bank. They offer comprehensive services but can be more strict with foreign applicants.

Japan Post Bank (Yucho Bank)

Highly popular among foreigners due to its widespread ATM network (often found in convenience stores) and generally simpler account opening process for new residents. Offers basic banking services.

Online Banks

Banks like Rakuten Bank and SBI Sumishin Net Bank offer convenient online services, often with lower fees. Account opening can be done remotely, but sometimes requires a Japanese phone number and address.

Regional Banks / Credit Unions

Smaller banks focused on specific prefectures or cities. May be easier to open an account if you're a long-term resident in their service area, but services might be limited outside that region.

Opening a Bank Account: Step-by-Step

1. Residency Requirement

You generally need to have resided in Japan for at least 6 months to open a full bank account. Some banks (like Japan Post Bank) may allow you to open a limited account (e.g., for receiving salary) before 6 months, which can be upgraded later.

2. Required Documents

While requirements vary slightly by bank, you will almost certainly need:

  • Residence Card (Zairyu Card): Your primary ID. Ensure your current address is updated on the back.
  • Passport: For identity verification.
  • Inkan/Hanko (Personal Seal): While not always mandatory, having one (especially for major banks) can simplify the process.
  • Phone Number: A Japanese mobile number is usually required.
  • Proof of Address: Sometimes required, e.g., utility bill or certificate of residence (juminhyo) from your ward office.
  • My Number Card (Individual Number Card): Increasingly requested, especially for tax-related purposes.

3. Visit a Bank Branch

It's highly recommended to visit a branch in person, ideally one that has English-speaking staff (especially in major city centers). The process is done face-to-face.

  • Be prepared for a lengthy process (1-2 hours) and potentially filling out forms in Japanese.
  • You will typically receive your cash card (ATM card) by mail a few days later.

Common Banking Services

ATMs (Cash Dispensers)

Widely available, but operating hours for deposits/withdrawals can be limited outside of business hours, and fees may apply. Convenience store ATMs (Seven Bank, E-net, Lawson Bank) are often 24/7 and more foreigner-friendly.

Online Banking / Mobile Apps

Most major banks offer online banking and mobile apps, though their English support varies. These are essential for managing transfers, checking balances, and paying bills efficiently.

International Transfers

Traditional banks can be expensive for international transfers. Many foreigners use dedicated international money transfer services like Wise (formerly TransferWise) or Revolut for better rates and lower fees.

Credit Cards

Obtaining a Japanese credit card can be challenging for new residents without a credit history. Many start with a debit card or a prepaid card. Foreign credit cards are widely accepted in major cities, but cash is still king in many smaller establishments.

Important Tips for Banking in Japan

Language Barrier: Be prepared for limited English support, especially outside major cities. Bring a Japanese-speaking friend or use translation apps if needed.

Patience is Key: Banking procedures can be slow and meticulous. Allocate ample time for visits to the bank.

Inkan/Hanko: While not always strictly required for opening, having a personal seal can make many future transactions (like signing contracts) smoother.

Online Banking: Even if challenging initially, setting up online banking is highly recommended for convenience.

For official guidance, refer to the websites of individual banks or seek advice from expat communities in Japan.
