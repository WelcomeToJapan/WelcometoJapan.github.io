Kanji: The Key to Japanese Mastery

Though daunting at first, Kanji are extremely helpful and can rapidly improve your comprehension.

Unlocking Deeper Meaning with Kanji

Kanji are Chinese characters adopted into the Japanese writing system, representing concepts or ideas.

After mastering Hiragana and Katakana, Kanji is the next step in becoming truly literate in Japanese. Each Kanji character carries its own meaning and can have multiple readings depending on context and combination with other characters.

While learning thousands of Kanji can seem daunting, starting with common ones and understanding their components (radicals) can make the process more manageable and rewarding. Kanji helps to differentiate homophones and makes written Japanese more concise and easier to read.

Key Aspects of Kanji

1. Readings: On'yomi and Kun'yomi

Kanji typically have two types of readings:

  • On'yomi (音読み): Chinese-derived readings, often used when Kanji are combined with other Kanji (e.g., 日本 - にほん "Nihon" - Japan).
  • Kun'yomi (訓読み): Native Japanese readings, often used when Kanji stand alone or are followed by Hiragana (e.g., 食べる - たべる "taberu" - to eat).

2. Stroke Order

Writing Kanji correctly involves following a specific stroke order. This is important not just for aesthetics, but also for memorization and recognition. Consistent stroke order also helps with faster and more legible writing.

General rules include writing from top to bottom, left to right, and horizontal strokes before vertical ones.

3. Radicals (部首 - Bushu)

Kanji are often composed of smaller components called radicals. Understanding common radicals can help you guess the meaning or categorize a Kanji, even if you don't know the full character.
