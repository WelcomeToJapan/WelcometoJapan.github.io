Budgeting for Your Japan Trip

A real-world guide to costs for flights, food, hotels, and train passes.

One of the biggest questions when traveling overseas is: "How much will it cost?" Fortunately, Japan is surprisingly affordable, especially as the Yen continues to plummet. It CAN, though, also be very expensive - it all depends on your travel style. This guide will give you some idea of what to expect.

Detailed Cost Breakdown

Here’s a closer look at where your money will go. Note that these are estimates and can vary widely based on the season and where you go. Kyoto, for example, will always be more expensive than Nagano or Nagoya. Tokyo or Sapporo will cost more than Nagoya or Kobe.

Accommodation

  • Hostels: ¥3,000 - ¥5,000 per night for a dorm bed.
  • Business Hotels: ¥8,000 - ¥15,000 per night for a clean, private room.
  • Mid-Range Hotels / Ryokan: ¥20,000 - ¥40,000 per night.
  • Luxury Stays: ¥50,000+ per night.

Food & Drink

You can eat very well in Japan without spending a fortune.

  • Budget Meals: ¥600 - ¥1,200 (Ramen, gyudon, convenience store meals).
  • Mid-Range Restaurant Meal: ¥1,500 - ¥3,000.
  • High-End Dinner / Kaiseki: ¥10,000+.
  • Budget Tip: Plan for around ¥4,000 - ¥6,000 per person per day for a good mix of cheap eats and one nice restaurant meal.

Transportation

  • Local Transit: Budget about ¥800 - ¥1,500 per day for subways and buses in a major city.
  • Shinkansen (one-way): Tokyo to Kyoto costs around ¥14,000.
  • 7-Day JR Pass: ¥50,000. As discussed in our transportation guide, this is only worth it if you're taking multiple long-distance trips.

Activities & Sightseeing

  • Temples & Shrines: Many are free, but famous ones often charge ¥300 - ¥600 for entry.
  • Museums: Typically ¥600 - ¥2,000.
  • Observation Decks (e.g., Shibuya Sky): Around ¥2,000 - ¥3,000.

My Top Money-Saving Tips

Having lived here for years, here are some real tips to make your money go further:

  • Love the Konbini: Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) are everywhere, and they are everyone's best friend. They have good-quality sandwiches, filling onigiri (rice balls), and even hot meals (heated in the Konbini microwave) for a few hundred yen. And then there is the Oden and fried food at the counter, as well as the famous FamiChiki!
  • Lunch Sets are a Bargain: Many restaurants offer a *teishoku* (set lunch) for around ¥1,000, which would cost double or triple at dinner time. Look for "teishoku" on menus, as these include sides.
  • Travel Off-Season: Avoid late March/early April (cherry blossoms) and Golden Week (late April/early May) when prices for flights and hotels skyrocket.
  • Use Highway Buses: For city-to-city travel without a JR Pass, night buses are slow but cheap, and can save you a night's accommodation cost.
  • Drink Tap Water: It's perfectly safe and clean everywhere in Japan. Bring a reusable bottle.
