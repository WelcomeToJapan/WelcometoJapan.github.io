Housing in Japan

Your essential guide to navigating the rental market and finding your home in Japan.

Finding Your Place in the Land of the Rising Sun

Understanding the Japanese housing market is key to a comfortable life as a resident.

Finding suitable housing in Japan, especially as a foreigner, can be a challenging but rewarding experience. The rental market has its unique customs, fees, and types of accommodation that differ significantly from many Western countries.

This guide will walk you through the common types of housing, the typical rental process, associated costs, and tips for a smoother search to help you settle into your new life in Japan.

Common Types of Housing

Apartments (Aparto / Manshon)

The most common type of housing. "Aparto" refers to wooden/light-steel frame buildings, typically 1-2 stories. "Manshon" are reinforced concrete buildings, usually 3+ stories, offering more amenities.

Share Houses / Guest Houses

Popular among students and young professionals. You get a private room but share common areas (kitchen, bathroom) with other residents. Often furnished and require less initial money.

Dormitories (Gakusei Ryō)

University or company-managed accommodation for students or employees. Often the most affordable option, but with strict rules and limited privacy.

Single-Family Homes (Ikkenya)

Less common for renters, especially in major cities, but available in suburban or rural areas. Offers more space and privacy but can be more expensive and harder to find.

The Japanese Rental Process

1. Search for Properties

Start your search online using websites like SUUMO, HOME'S, or GaijinPot Apartments. For a smoother experience, consider real estate agents specializing in foreigners (e.g., Leopalace21, Oakhouse for share houses).

2. Viewings & Application

Once you find properties of interest, arrange viewings. If you like a place, you'll submit an application form. Be prepared to provide personal details, employment information, and sometimes a guarantor.

3. Guarantor (Hoshōnin)

Most landlords require a guarantor. If you don't have a Japanese guarantor, you'll likely need to use a guarantor company (hoshō kaisha), which charges a fee (usually 50-100% of one month's rent).

4. Contract & Initial Costs

Upon approval, you'll sign the rental contract. Be prepared for significant upfront costs, which typically include:

  • Rent: 1 month in advance.
  • Deposit (Shikikin): 1-2 months' rent (refundable, but often partially deducted for cleaning/repairs).
  • Key Money (Reikin): 0-2 months' rent (non-refundable payment to the landlord).
  • Agent Fee (Chūkai Tesūryō): 0.5-1 month's rent + consumption tax.
  • Guarantor Company Fee: As mentioned above.
  • Lock Exchange Fee: Around ¥15,000-¥25,000.
  • Fire Insurance: Mandatory, usually ¥15,000-¥20,000 for two years.

5. Move-in

Once all payments are made and the contract is signed, you'll receive the keys and can move in. It's common for apartments to be unfurnished, so factor in furniture costs.

Important Tips for Renters

Be Patient: The process can take time, especially if you're new to Japan. Start your search well in advance.

Budget Carefully: Initial costs are high. Ensure you have sufficient funds saved up.

Language Barrier: While some agents cater to foreigners, knowing some basic Japanese or having a Japanese-speaking friend can be very helpful.

Read the Contract: Understand all terms and conditions, especially regarding cleaning fees and deposit refunds, before signing.

Utilities: Remember to set up electricity, gas, and water accounts upon moving in. Internet setup can also take time.

For detailed information and assistance, consider consulting real estate agencies that specialize in helping foreigners find housing in Japan.
