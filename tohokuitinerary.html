Explore Tohoku: A 10-Day Adventure

Discover the true Japan in its less-traveled northern regions.

The Tohoku region, the northeastern part of Japan's main island of Honshu, is a land of dramatic landscapes, warm hospitality, and a unique form of Japanese culture (this site’s creator has lived here for nearly two decades - it’s fantastic!). This itinerary will take you from samurai towns and historic temples to pristine lakes and traditional hot springs, showcasing the best of this less-traveled, yet incredibly rewarding, part of Japan.

Before You Go:

Transportation: The JR East-Tohoku Area Pass (valid for 5 flexible days within a 14-day period) is highly recommended and more cost-effective than a national Japan Rail Pass for this specific itinerary. For ultimate flexibility, especially for accessing remote nature spots like Oirase Stream or Ouchijuku, consider renting a car.

Best Time to Visit:

  • Spring (late April - May): Stunning cherry blossoms, especially in Hirosaki and Kakunodate.
  • Summer (July - August): Lush greenery and vibrant festivals, like Aomori's Nebuta Matsuri.
  • Autumn (October - November): Breathtaking fall colors, arguably the most beautiful season in Tohoku.
  • Winter (December - February): A snow-covered wonderland, famous for onsen and snow monsters. For winter, see our separate itinerary or our Winter Sports section.

Accommodation: Depending when and where you go, you should book your stays in advance, especially for popular onsen towns like Ginzan Onsen. The traditional ryokans (inns) fill up quickly, but if you have a car you gain access to all sorts of off-the-beaten track places to stay, and online reservation sites will usually have something available.

The 10-Day Tohoku Adventure Itinerary

Day 1: Getting to Tohoku - Sendai (Miyagi Prefecture)

Focus: Arrival and exploring Sendai's history.

Morning: Activate your JR Pass and take the Hayabusa Shinkansen from Tokyo to Sendai (approx. 90 minutes). Check into your hotel and drop off your luggage.

Afternoon: Visit the ruins of Aoba Castle. While the main keep is gone, the site offers a commanding panoramic view of the city and a famous statue of the founding feudal lord, Date Masamune. Afterwards, visit the ornate Zuihoden Mausoleum, the final resting place of Masamune and his heirs.

Evening: Try Sendai's most famous culinary specialty: Gyutan (grilled beef tongue). Explore the covered shopping arcades of Ichibancho for dinner and shopping.

Stay: Sendai

Day 2: Heavenly Islands of Matsushima Bay (Miyagi Prefecture)

Focus: Scenic beauty and coastal temples.

Morning: Take a short train ride on the JR Senseki Line to Matsushima-Kaigan Station (approx. 40 minutes). Take a sightseeing cruise around Matsushima Bay, famous for its 260 pine-clad islets and considered one of Japan's Three Most Scenic Views.

Afternoon: Explore the sights on shore. Visit Zuiganji Temple, one of the most important Zen temples in northern Japan. Cross the iconic red bridge to Godaido Hall, a small temple hall on an islet. Enjoy fresh oysters and other seafood from the local stalls.

Evening: Return to Sendai for the night.

Stay: Sendai

Day 3: Mountain Temple & Relaxing Onsen (Yamagata Prefecture)

Focus: Spiritual climb and traditional hot spring experience.

Morning: Travel from Sendai to Yamadera Station (approx. 1 hour on the JR Senzan Line). Climb the 1,015 stone steps to the magnificent Risshakuji Temple (Yamadera), which clings to the mountainside and offers incredible views of the valley below.

Afternoon: Travel from Yamadera to Oishida Station, then take a 35-minute bus ride to Ginzan Onsen. This exquisitely preserved hot spring town, with its wooden ryokan lining a central river, feels like stepping into a Studio Ghibli film. In fact, if you’ve seen Spirited Away you’ll recognise some of it!

Evening: Check into a traditional ryokan. Put on your yukata and stroll through the gas-lit streets after enjoying a multi-course kaiseki dinner.

Stay: Ginzan Onsen (or nearby onsen resorts - there are many great ones, though Japanese language skills are helpful for booking).

Day 4: Samurai District in Kakunodate (Akita Prefecture)

Focus: Preserved samurai history and traditional architecture.

Morning: Enjoy a final soak in the onsen before taking the bus and train combination from Ginzan Onsen to Kakunodate (via Oishida and Shinjo/Omono Stations, or back via Yamagata Shinkansen lines). This will be a scenic travel morning.

Afternoon: Explore Kakunodate, one of Japan's best-preserved samurai towns. Walk through the main Samurai District, where grand samurai manors like the Aoyagi and Ishiguro houses are open to the public. The streets are famously lined with weeping cherry trees.

Culinary Highlight: Try Akita's Inaniwa Udon, a type of thin and smooth udon noodle.

Stay: Kakunodate

Day 5: UNESCO Temples & Noodle Challenge (Iwate Prefecture)

Focus: Ancient history and a unique culinary experience.

Morning: Take the Akita Shinkansen from Kakunodate to Morioka (approx. 1 hour). Store your luggage at Morioka Station and take a local train south to Hiraizumi (approx. 1 hour).

Afternoon: Rent a bicycle near Hiraizumi Station to explore its UNESCO World Heritage sites. The highlight is Chuson-ji Temple, home to the incredible Konjikido (Golden Hall), a small hall completely covered in gold leaf. Also visit the beautiful gardens of Motsu-ji Temple.

Evening: Return to Morioka. For dinner, take the Wanko Soba challenge—a unique all-you-can-eat experience where servers continuously place small bowls of soba noodles into your bowl until you surrender by putting a lid on it (I gave up embarrassingly quickly).

Stay: Morioka

Day 6: Pristine Nature (Aomori Prefecture)

Focus: Hiking along a beautiful mountain stream.

Morning: From Morioka, take the Shinkansen to Hachinohe Station. From there, board a JR bus bound for Lake Towada and get off along the Oirase Stream. (Note: The JR bus is covered by the national JR Pass, but sometimes not by the JR East Tohoku Pass; check terms or pay separately).

Afternoon: Walk or rent a bicycle to explore the Oirase Stream, a crystal-clear mountain stream that flows through a beautiful deciduous forest. The path is dotted with dozens of picturesque waterfalls and rapids. It’s about 14 km long, so cover a section that suits your energy level.

Evening: The bus continues to the shores of Lake Towada. Check into your hotel or ryokan.

Stay: Lake Towada

Day 7: Majestic Lake & Apple Pie (Aomori Prefecture)

Focus: Lake views and historic castle exploration.

Morning: Explore Lake Towada, a massive caldera lake with deep blue water. Take a sightseeing boat for a different perspective of the volcanic crater. Visit the serene Towada Shrine.

Afternoon: Take a bus from Lake Towada to Hirosaki. Upon arrival, head straight for Hirosaki Castle, one0 of the few castles in Japan with its original keep intact from the Edo Period. The surrounding park is spectacular, especially during cherry blossom or autumn foliage season.

Evening: Hirosaki is in the heart of Japan's apple country. Be sure to try some of its famous apple pie or fresh apple cider.

Stay: Hirosaki

Day 8: Festival Spirit & Ancient History (Aomori Prefecture)

Focus: Cultural insights and ancient Japanese civilization.

Morning: Take a short train ride to Aomori City. Start your day with a Nokkedon breakfast at the Furukawa Fish Market. Buy a sheet of tickets and wander the stalls, exchanging tickets for fresh seafood, rice, and soup to create your own custom seafood bowl.

Afternoon: Learn about Aomori's famous summer festival at the Nebuta Museum WA-RASSE, which displays the giant, illuminated paper floats used in the parade. Afterwards, visit the Sannai Maruyama Special Historical Site, an expansive and fascinating reconstruction of a large settlement from the ancient Jomon Period (c. 13,000 - 300 BC).

Stay: Aomori City

Day 9: Samurai Stronghold of Aizu (Fukushima Prefecture)

Focus: Exploring samurai history and resilience.

Morning: This is a major travel day. Take the Shinkansen from Shin-Aomori all the way south to Koriyama Station (approx. 3 hours). From there, transfer to a local line to Aizuwakamatsu (approx. 1 hour), a city with a tragic and heroic samurai history.

Afternoon: Visit Tsuruga Castle, a beautiful reconstruction with its unique red-tiled roof. Learn about the Byakkotai (White Tiger Force), a group of young teenage samurai who committed ritual suicide here during the Boshin War.

Evening: Relax in your hotel or consider staying in the nearby Higashiyama Onsen area for another hot spring experience.

Stay: Aizuwakamatsu

Day 10: Thatched Roof Village & Return to Tokyo

Focus: Stepping back in time and departure.

Morning: Drive, or take a train and bus combination to visit Ouchijuku, a former post town from the Edo Period. The main street is lined with traditional thatched-roof buildings, which now house shops, restaurants, and small inns, transporting you back in time.

Culinary Highlight: Try the local specialty, negi-soba, where you use a large green onion as your chopsticks to eat soba noodles.

Afternoon: Travel from Ouchijuku back to Aizuwakamatsu, and then begin your journey back to Tokyo via Koriyama Station (approx. 3 hours total).

Stay: N/A (Travel Day)
