Numbers in Japanese

From one to one million, a simple guide to mastering the Japanese counting system.

Learning to count is a fundamental step in any language. Luckily, the Japanese number system is incredibly logical and consistent. Once you learn the first ten numbers, you'll be able to count up to 99 with ease. Let's get started!

The Basics: 1 to 10

Everything starts here. Memorize these ten numbers, and the rest will fall into place. Note that some numbers have multiple readings, which are used in different contexts, but you can start with the most common ones.

Counting from 11 to 99

This is where the logic begins. To make numbers from 11 to 19, you simply say "ten" (juu) and add the second number. For multiples of ten, you say the number *then* "ten."

  • 11 is 10 + 1 → juu ichi
  • 14 is 10 + 4 → juu yon
  • 20 is 2 x 10 → ni juu
  • 30 is 3 x 10 → san juu
  • 56 is (5 x 10) + 6 → go juu roku

Bigger Numbers: Hundreds, Thousands, and Beyond

The system continues with new words for larger values. The most important one to learn is man (ten thousand), as this is where Japanese counting differs most from English.

Number Kanji Reading (Romaji)
100百hyaku
1,000千sen
10,000万man
100,000,000億oku

The Concept of 万 (man)

In English, we count in thousands (1,000), ten thousands (10,000), hundred thousands (100,000). In Japan, the system groups by ten thousands. After you reach 9,999, the next unit is man.

  • 10,000 is ichi man
  • 20,000 is ni man (two ten-thousands)
  • 100,000 is juu man (ten ten-thousands)

Putting It All Together: Examples

Let's look at some examples to see how it works.

  • 425 → 4-hundreds + 2-tens + 5 → yon hyaku ni juu go
  • 1,984 → 1-thousand + 9-hundreds + 8-tens + 4 → sen kyuu hyaku hachi juu yon
  • 56,789 → 5-ten-thousands + 6-thousands + 7-hundreds + 8-tens + 9 → go man roku sen nana hyaku hachi juu kyuu
